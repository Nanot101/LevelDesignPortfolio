Necrobotics

A deep dive into my level design process for this project.

Project Overview

For Necrobotics, I served as the Level Designer on an 8-member team. My primary responsibility was to design the world and player exploration flow for this exploration-driven game. The project featured minimal narrative, so I leaned on using environmental storytelling and game mechanics to guide players toward the final boss encounter.

Design Constraints

  • 1 Boss Encounter
  • 4 Player Abilities (1 Core + 3 Secondary)
  • 3 Sub-Sections / Zones
  • 3 Enemy Types
  • Dedicated Rest Areas for Pacing

Design Process

I began by mapping out player paths to ensure that core abilities were introduced in a safe, low-risk environment. To encourage exploration, I implemented soft locks that required players to collect abilities naturally before reaching the boss room.

I began by creating the first draft layout, combining the individual rooms into a cohesive flow. My initial focus was on defining entrances and exits to establish how players would naturally navigate between spaces. From there, I designed the first few rooms to:

  1. Introduce the core controls in a safe, guided environment
  2. Gradually present threats and environmental hazards
  3. Lead players toward the first challenge through clear, linear level design

Iteration & Modular Design

After playtesting the first few rooms, I moved on to design the rest of the rooms. Here was what the original layout looked like:

To keep the pacing engaging, I mixed intense sections with quieter moments by clustering enemies in specific areas and leaving other spots mostly empty.

However, we were falling behind schedule so we decided to scope down the game. Because I designed the world with modular rooms and branching paths, I was able to remove rooms without breaking progression. This ensured the final layout felt cohesive despite reduced content.

Original blockout (left) vs final scoped layout (right). Below is the final design in Godot.

Design Takeaways

  • Player-Centric Design: Introduced abilities and hazards naturally without forced tutorials.
  • Pacing & Flow: Balanced combat intensity with quiet exploration spaces.
  • Modular Thinking: Built rooms to allow scope reduction without breaking progression.
  • Iterative Workflow: Applied playtesting feedback to adjust layouts and encounters.
  • Collaboration: Gained experience in a Scrum-based, multi-disciplinary team.

Overall, Necrobotics strengthened my ability to design exploration-focused levels, iterate quickly, and adapt to production constraints. I’m eager to apply what I learned in a professional design team.
